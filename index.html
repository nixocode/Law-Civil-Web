§ I

Concept of Law — El Derecho

▾
📌 Summary
  • Law (El Derecho) is polysemic — best understood as the obligatory order of social relations.
  • Three purposes: Order · Justice · Legal Certainty (Seguridad Jurídica, Art. 9.3 CE).
  • The legal norm (norma jurídica) is its basic unit — external, objective, obligatory and coercible.
  • Unlike moral norms, legal norms depend on behavior, not internal intention.

Law has a polysemic meaning covering a plurality of realities. The best study-oriented definition: obligatory order in social relations. Law structures the social group — creates links, delimits permitted and prohibited behaviors, and establishes rights and obligations.

Three purposes of Law: (1) Guarantee order; (2) Ensure justice — protecting freedom, property, contracts; (3) Provide legal certainty (seguridad jurídica) so parties can foresee legal consequences. Essential for market confidence.

La Norma Jurídica

The basic unit of law. A mandate of conduct indicating what must/can/cannot be done. External and objective character — depends on behavior, not intention. Both obligatory and coercible: if not followed, the State can impose consequences (sanctions, nullity, compensation).

§ II

Classification of Law — Clasificación

▾
📌 Summary
  • Public Law: State exercises superior authority — no equality between parties. Includes Constitutional, Administrative, Criminal, Tax Law.
  • Private Law: individuals relate on a basis of legal equality. Foundation: autonomía de la voluntad. Includes Civil + Commercial Law.
  • Criminal Law = Public: State's ius puniendi protects general social interest, not just the victim. Penalties cannot be "negotiated away."

Public Law — Derecho Público

Regulates the State and State–citizen relations when the Administration acts in the exercise of its authority. One party is always a public body acting with superior powers — no equality between parties.

ConstitutionalAdministrativeCriminalTaxPublic International
Why Criminal Law is Public: Protects the general interest of society through the State's ius puniendi (right to punish). No one can "negotiate" a penalty — criminal law is imperative.

Private Law — Derecho Privado

Regulates relations between private subjects on a basis of legal equality. Foundation: autonomía de la voluntad — parties freely organize their relationships within legal limits. Mandatory rules protect vulnerable parties (consumers, workers).

Sources of Law — Fuentes del Derecho

▾
📌 Summary
  • Spain uses a hierarchy: Constitution → EU Law → Law → Regulation. Below these, subsidiary sources fill gaps.
  • Subsidiary (apply only when no law exists): Custom (costumbre) + General Principles of Law.
  • Auxiliary (interpret, don't create): Jurisprudence (Supreme Court) + Doctrine.
  • EU Regulations = directly applicable. EU Directives = require national transposition.
  • Custom needs: repeated practice + opinio iuris (belief it's legally obligatory).

The Legal Relationship — La Relación Jurídica

▾
📌 Summary
  • Legal relationship = legally recognized bond between subjects from which rights and duties derive.
  • 4 structural elements: Subjective (who) · Objective (what) · Legal Nexus (why) · Temporal (when).
  • Object of obligation: Dar (give/deliver) · Hacer (do/perform) · No hacer (abstain).
  • Prescripción (inactivity extinguishes action) vs. Caducidad (non-extendable period lapses) — both serve legal certainty.

Organization of the State — Organización del Estado

▾
📌 Summary
  • Art. 1.1 CE: Spain = "social and democratic State governed by the rule of law" — three distinct pillars.
  • Three Powers: Legislative (Cortes Generales) · Executive (Gobierno) · Judicial (Jueces y Tribunales). Functional separation with mutual control.
  • Estado Autonómico: 17 Autonomous Communities + local entities. Conflicts by principle of competence, not hierarchy.
  • Constitutional Court = guardian of constitutional supremacy + recurso de amparo.

Art. 1.1 CE — Three Pillars

PillarSpanishWhat it means

Democratic StateEstado DemocráticoPower from popular will. Citizens elect representatives. Political pluralism (Art. 1.1 CE)

Three Powers — Los Tres Poderes

  • Legislative — Cortes Generales: Congress + Senate. Drafts/approves laws, controls Government, approves Budget.
  • Executive — El Gobierno: Directs policy, manages Administration, applies laws, issues regulations. Decree-Laws in urgent cases.
  • Judicial — Jueces y Tribunales: Judges and enforces law. Constitutionally independent, irremovable, subject only to the rule of law.

Territorial Organization — Estado Autonómico

Art. 2 CE: unity of the Spanish Nation. But recognizes the right to autonomy of nationalities and regions. 17 CCAAs governed by their Estatuto de Autonomía. Key: competence principle (not hierarchy) resolves State vs. CCAA conflicts. Constitutional Court = territorial guarantor.

§ I

Civil Law — Derecho Civil

▾
📌 Summary
  • Civil Law: branch of private law regulating the legal life of individuals (persons, contracts, property, family) without requiring special status.
  • Spanish Civil Code (1889): Preliminary Title + 4 Books. Core text of common civil law. Many areas now in special laws (descodificación).
  • Foral rights (Derechos Forales): special civil laws in Aragon, Balearics, Catalonia, Galicia, Navarre, Basque Country. Apply preferentially; CC fills gaps subsidiarily.
  • Catalan CC (CCC): 6 books, modern structure. Key difference: defaults to separation of property vs. CC's community of property.
  • Art. 149.1.8ª CE: State has exclusive civil competence, but CCAAs can preserve, modify and develop pre-existing civil law.

Content of Civil Law

Civil Law covers: (1) legal regime of the person (personality, capacity, civil status); (2) obligations and contracts; (3) rights in rem (derechos reales) — direct power over assets, erga omnes; (4) Land Registry — real estate legal publicity; (5) Family Law — marriage, filiation, parental authority, protection of minors.

The Codification Process

Codification = unifying dispersed norms into a single coherent text. In Europe, 19th-century movement inspired by the French Civil Code of 1804 (Napoleonic Code). In Spain, process started at the Cortes de Cádiz (1812). Full unification failed because of pre-existing territorial civil systems. Result: dual system — Civil Code (1889) + foral rights for certain territories.

Foral Rights (Derechos Forales): Special civil law of Aragon, Balearics, Catalonia, Galicia, Navarre, Basque Country. "Foral" from fuero — medieval royal privileges. Apply preferentially; CC fills gaps subsidiarily.

Post-codification trend: descodificación — matters progressively removed from the CC into special laws (reinforced by EU integration).

Structure of the Civil Code

  • Preliminary Title: Sources of law, interpretation, application, effectiveness.
  • Book 1: Persons — personality, civil status, family, marriage, filiation, protection of minors.
  • Book 2: Goods, property and modifications.
  • Book 3: Ways of acquiring property (occupation, donation, succession).
  • Book 4: Obligations and contracts — most active book in daily practice.

§ II

Rights of the Person — Derechos de la Persona

▾
📌 Summary
  • Legal personality: acquired at birth (Art. 30 CC), ends at death (Art. 32 CC). Unborn child has conditional protection (Art. 29 CC).
  • Law 8/2021: abolished judicial incapacitation. Everyone has legal capacity on equal terms. System now provides support measures — not replacements for the person's will.
  • Age of majority: 18 (Art. 240 CC). Emancipation from 16 — partial autonomy, but Art. 247 CC restricts major financial acts.
  • Personality rights: life, integrity, honour, privacy, image, name. Absolute rights — erga omnes. Triple protection: constitutional + criminal + civil (LO 1/1982).
  • Civil status elements: Nationality · Vecindad Civil · Domicile — determine applicable law and legal position.

The Natural Person

Legal personality = the legal recognition of existence as a subject of law. Acquired at birth (Art. 30 CC — even for an instant). Art. 29 CC: "birth determines personality, but the conceived child is considered born for all purposes that are favourable to him, provided he is born alive."

Legal personality ends at death (Art. 32 CC). Death triggers hereditary succession — estate and legal relationships transfer to heirs.

Law 8/2021 — The Capacity Reform (IMPORTANT)

The big change: Spain abandoned the old incapacitation model. The new system: everyone has legal capacity on equal terms. Where needed: support measures that assist, not replace, the person's will. Key principle: proportional, individualized, reviewable, respect for the person's will, desires, and preferences.

Four support categories: (1) Voluntary measures — person designates their own support in advance; (2) De facto guardianship (guarda de hecho) — informal family support, now a recognized legal institution; (3) Curatela (Guardianship) — main judicial support; replaces old conservatorship; generally assists, only exceptionally represents; (4) Judicial defender (defensor judicial) — ad hoc for specific conflicts or interests.

Minority & Emancipation

Age of majority: 18 (Art. 240 CC). During minority, parental authority (patria potestad, Art. 154 CC) governs. Must always be exercised for the child's benefit.

Emancipation (Art. 241 CC): from age 16, by parental grant (public deed or Civil Registry) or judicial grant. Art. 247 CC restriction: cannot borrow money, encumber or sell real estate, or alienate extraordinary-value goods without parental consent.

Personality Rights

Rights falling on the person's own sphere (physical and mental). (a) Affect the person's own sphere; (b) absolute rights — duty of general respect from all (Art. 9.1 CE); (c) inherent to human dignity (Art. 10.1 CE). Triple protection: constitutional + criminal + civil (LO 1/1982).

  • Right to life (Art. 15 CE)
  • Right to physical and moral integrity — no torture or degrading treatment (Art. 15 CE)
  • Rights to honour, privacy, and own image (Art. 18 CE + LO 1/1982)
  • Right to a name (Civil Registry Law Art. 11): freedom of choice of proper name, with limits (no names contrary to dignity, no same name as living sibling with identical surnames)

§ III

Civil Contracts — Contratos Civiles

▾
📌 Summary
  • Obligation: creditor demands performance from debtor. Object = Dar/Hacer/No hacer. 5 sources: law, contracts, quasi-contracts, crimes, civil torts.
  • Contract (Art. 1254 CC): agreement of wills. 3 essential elements (Art. 1261 CC): consent + object + cause. Based on autonomía de la voluntad (Art. 1255 CC).
  • Breach types: total non-performance · defective · late. Attributable (dolo/culpa) = liability. Non-attributable (fortuitous / force majeure) = possible exoneration.
  • Invalidity vs. Voidability vs. Rescission: key exam topic — different defects, deadlines, who can invoke, whether curable.
  • Freedom of form (Art. 1278 CC) — general rule. Exception: some need public deed for validity (forma ad solemnitatem).

Law of Obligations — Derecho de Obligaciones

Every obligation has: Creditor (demands) · Debtor (must comply) · Object (dar/hacer/no hacer — must be lawful, determined, possible) · Legal nexus. 5 sources of obligations: law; legal transactions/contracts; quasi-contracts (unilateral will — undue payment, management of another's affairs); crimes (Penal Code governs); civil torts (acts/omissions with fault/negligence causing damage).

Breach types: Dolo = deliberate (max liability). Culpa = lacks diligence without intent. Caso fortuito / Fuerza mayor = external, unforeseeable → debtor may be exonerated. Exception: if debtor was already in unjustified delay, force majeure may not exonerate.

Essential Elements (Art. 1261 CC)

  • Consent: free manifestation of will. Vices (error, fraud, violence, intimidation) → voidability.
  • Object: possible, lawful, determined or determinable. Unlawful/impossible → nullity.
  • Cause: economic/social function. Must exist, be lawful and true. Absence/falsity → nullity.

§ IV

Rights in Rem — Derechos Reales

▾
📌 Summary
  • Rights in rem: direct and immediate power over a thing, enforceable erga omnes (against everyone). No need for another person's cooperation.
  • Property (Art. 348 CC): fullest real right — use, enjoy, dispose. Not absolute: social function (Art. 33 CE), no abuse of rights (Art. 7 CC).
  • Limited real rights: Preferential acquisition (tanteo, retracto, opción) · Enjoyment (usufruct, use, habitation, easements) · Guarantee (pledge, mortgage).
  • Mortgage: registration in Land Registry is constitutive (Art. 1875 CC) — no valid mortgage without it.
  • Land Registry: public faith, priority, rogation, successive tract, speciality. Art. 34 LH protects the third party in good faith.

Property — Propiedad (Arts. 348–429 CC)

Full right in rem: use, enjoy, and dispose within legal limits. Subject to: Art. 7 CC (no abuse of rights), Art. 33 CE (social function — expropriation allowed for justified cause with prior compensation). Ways to acquire (Art. 609 CC): occupation, law, donation, succession mortis causa, tradition (via contracts), usucapion.

Preferential Acquisition Rights

  • Tanteo (right of first refusal): exercise BEFORE the transfer — owner must notify holder; holder can match the offer.
  • Retracto (Art. 1518 CC): exercise AFTER the transfer — subrogate in buyer's position, reimburse price + expenses.
  • Opción de compra: unilateral right to decide whether to enter a pre-configured sale within a period. Registrable → erga omnes.

Key Enjoyment Rights

  • Usufruct (Arts. 467–522 CC): broadest. Use + all fruits. Owner retains nuda propiedad. Temporary: life (natural person) or max 30 years (legal person).
  • Right of Use: only what's needed for personal/family needs. Non-transferable, non-seizeable.
  • Right of Habitation: occupy necessary rooms for personal residence only. Non-transferable.
  • Easements (Arts. 530–604 CC): burden on finca sirviente for benefit of finca dominante. E.g., rights of way, lights and views, water.

Guarantee Rights

  • Pledge — Prenda (Arts. 1863–1873 CC): movable property. Possessory displacement required (thing given to creditor). Indivisible. No pacto comisorio (creditor cannot appropriate).
  • Mortgage — Hipoteca (Arts. 1874–1880 CC + LH): immovable property. No possessory displacement. Registration in Land Registry is constitutive (Art. 1875 CC). Requires public deed + registration.

Land Registry — Registro de la Propiedad

Public body for registering ownership and real rights over real estate. Main purpose: real estate legal publicity. Distinct from Cadastre (Catastro): Registry = legal ownership/rights; Cadastre = physical/tax description.

Registry Principles: (1) Public faith — acquirer in good faith for consideration is protected; (2) Priority — first to register prevails; (3) Rogation — registration on request; (4) Successive tract — transferor's right must be registered first; (5) Speciality/Legality — each property perfectly individualised; registrar verifies validity.

Art. 34 LH — Tercero Hipotecario (Third Party in Good Faith): Acquirer who buys for consideration and in good faith, relying on the Registry, is protected even if the seller's title is later annulled. Requirements: onerous, good faith, reliance on Registry content, subsequent registration of acquired right.

Family Rights — Derecho de Familia

▾
📌 Summary
  • Family Law: mixed nature — Private Law but with mandatory rules limiting autonomía de la voluntad. Art. 39 CE mandates State protection of the family.
  • Marriage (Art. 45 CC): no marriage without freely given marital consent. Impediments: unemancipated minors, existing marriage, blood kinship to 3rd degree.
  • Filiation (Art. 108 CC): all children equal before law regardless of origin. By nature (marital/non-marital) or adoption.
  • Maintenance (alimentos, Art. 142 CC): covers food, housing, clothing, medical care, education. Proportional to resources and needs.
  • Matrimonial regimes: Common law default = gananciales. Catalonia/Balearics = separación de bienes. Couples choose via capitulaciones matrimoniales.

Marriage — El Matrimonio

Legal union of two people via freely given consent (Art. 45 CC). Bilateral and formal legal transaction — must be registered in Civil Registry. Personal effects: cohabitation, fidelity, mutual help. Patrimonial effects: triggers matrimonial property regime.

Impediments (Arts. 46–47 CC): unemancipated minors; existing valid marriage (monogamy); relatives in direct line (consanguinity or adoption); collateral relatives up to 3rd degree by consanguinity; convicted as author/accomplice of the intentional death of the other's spouse.

De Facto Unions — Uniones de Hecho

Stable cohabitation without marriage. No unitary state regulation — fragmented through regional rules and case law. Supreme Court (12 Sept 2005): matrimonial regime cannot apply by analogy. Economic conflicts resolved via community of property, cohabitation agreements, or unjust enrichment principles.

Filiation (Art. 108 CC)

All children equal regardless of origin. By nature (marital — paternity presumption operates; non-marital — by recognition, judicial decision, or possession of status) or by adoption (fully integrates into adopter's family; ties with family of origin generally extinguished). Effects: surnames, parental authority, maintenance, inheritance rights.

Kinship & Maintenance (Arts. 142–153 CC)

Maintenance = food, housing, clothing, medical care, education (including of minor, or adult who hasn't finished studies for non-attributable reasons). Obligors: spouses, ascendants, descendants, siblings. Amount: proportional to obligor's resources + recipient's needs (Art. 146 CC). Modifiable if circumstances change.

Origin & Meaning of Commercial Law — Derecho Mercantil

▾
📌 Summary
  • Commercial Law (DM) is a branch of private law — a special law born because Civil Law was too slow for commercial traffic. Originally a law "of and for" merchants.
  • Origins: Late Middle Ages (11th century). Merchants in guilds created the Ius Mercatorum — applied by Consular Courts based on custom and good faith (ex aequo et bono).
  • 19th century codification: French Commercial Code (1807) shifted from a subjective (who is a merchant?) to an objective system (what is an act of commerce?). Spain's Commercial Code of 1885 (still in force) follows this hybrid approach.
  • Today: Commercial Law revolves around the company and the entrepreneur. Shaped by the Spanish Constitution (1978), EU integration, and antitrust law.

Why Commercial Law Exists

Commercial Law did not exist autonomously in Roman Law — the flexibility of the ius gentium and the praetor made it unnecessary. Its true origin lies in the Late Middle Ages, when the rise of cities and trade after feudalism created needs that Civil Law could not meet. Merchants in guilds (gremios) created their own law — the Ius Mercatorum — applied by Consular Courts (Consulados) based on commercial custom and equity.

Modern Age & Codification

In the 16th–18th centuries, absolute monarchs absorbed commercial law into national ordinances (e.g., the Ordenanzas de Bilbao in Spain). The French Revolution pushed for objective systems: the French Commercial Code (1807) based liability on the act of commerce, not on being a merchant. Spain's Commercial Code (1885) still in force follows this hybrid trend.

Spanish Commercial Code of 1885 (CCo): The core text of Spanish commercial law, still in force. It declares application to acts of commerce "whether or not they are merchants who execute them." Art. 1 defines the merchant as one who, with legal capacity, habitually engages in commerce in their own name.

Current Meaning — The Theory of the Company

In the 20th century, doctrine (led by Wieland) replaced the artificial concept of "act of commerce" with the theory of the company and entrepreneur. Today Commercial Law is understood as the law regulating the organization and professional activity of entrepreneurs in the market. Its scope extends from traditional traders to industry, services, and the primary sector. New challenges: the Spanish Constitution (Art. 38 CE — freedom of enterprise), EU harmonization, and antitrust (competition) legislation.

§ II

The Company & the Entrepreneur — Empresa y Empresario

▾
📌 Summary
  • The company (empresa) is an organization of personal, material, and intangible resources professionally directed to the market. Four elements: human factor · material/intangible assets · organization · environment.
  • The entrepreneur (empresario) organizes productive factors in their own name under risk. Four functions: Plan · Manage · Organize · Control.
  • Universal liability (Art. 1,911 CC): the debtor answers for obligations with all present and future assets. Scope depends on legal form adopted.
  • Limited Liability Entrepreneur (ERL), Law 14/2013: individual entrepreneurs can protect their main residence (up to €300,000 / €450,000 in large cities) if registered in the Commercial Registry.
  • Key distinction: sole proprietor (natural person, unlimited liability) vs. social entrepreneur (legal person — liability limited to company assets).

The Company as Economic & Legal Unit

The company is the fundamental engine of economic activity — an organization combining human, material, and intangible resources professionally directed to the market. Key intangibles: goodwill (fondo de comercio) — the value of organization above physical assets — and the customer base. Modern companies also pursue growth objectives and assume social/ethical responsibility toward stakeholders.

Entrepreneur — From Merchant to Risk Manager

The entrepreneur (natural or legal person) organizes productive factors in their own name and makes decisions under risky conditions. Risk can be economic (invested capital) or professional (management role). Four cyclical functions: Plan (analyse and set objectives) → Manage (direct organization toward goals) → Organize (coordinate resources) → Control (verify compliance and correct deviations).

Company Law — Derecho de Sociedades

▾
📌 Summary
  • Commercial companies are governed by numerus clausus — only the types the legislature creates exist: colectiva, comandita, SL, SA.
  • Lifting the veil (levantamiento del velo): courts can pierce the legal personality of a company if it is used for fraud or to evade the law.
  • Partnerships (sociedades personalistas): partners liable unlimitedly, jointly and severally. Key types: Colectiva (all partners liable) and Comandita (general partners unlimited, limited partners only up to their contribution).
  • Capital companies: SL (min. €3,000, shares, closed — transfer restricted) and SA (min. €60,000, shares freely negotiable, open). Both under the TRLSC.
  • Commercial Registry: registration is constitutive for capital companies (SA/SL — they don't legally exist until registered). Mandatory acts include incorporation, bylaws changes, directors, annual accounts, and dissolution.

Partnerships — Sociedades Personalistas

Based on intuitu personae — the personal qualities of each partner are the determining cause. Partners directly manage and assume unlimited liability.

  • Colectiva (General Partnership): all partners manage and are liable unlimitedly, jointly, and severally (though subsidiarily — company assets must be exhausted first). A non-compete prohibition applies. Industrial partners (contributing work) share profits proportionally to the smallest capitalist partner.
  • Comandita (Limited Partnership): coexistence of general partners (manage, unlimited liability) and limited partners (liability limited to their contribution, cannot manage). If a limited partner includes their name in the company name, they become liable as a general partner.

Special Commercial Companies

  • SAE (European Public Limited Company): for companies operating across EU Member States. Min. capital €120,000.
  • Sociedades laborales: SA or SL where most capital is owned by workers with indefinite employment contracts.
  • Professional Societies: joint exercise of a profession requiring a university degree + professional association registration (lawyers, architects). Majority of capital/votes must belong to professional partners.
  • SLNE (New Company SL): simplified SL for quick electronic formation of small companies.
  • Sole proprietorship company: SA or SL with one single partner. Subject to strict publicity requirements.

Commercial Registry — Registro Mercantil

Main function: legal publicity of commercial entrepreneurs' legal situations, providing security to third parties. Key distinction: registration is constitutive for capital companies (SA/SL — no legal personality without it), but merely declarative for partnerships. Mandatory registered acts: incorporation (public deed within 2 months), bylaws amendments, appointment/dismissal of directors + auditors, annual accounts deposit (balance sheet, P&L, report — penalty: registry closure), structural modifications (merger, spin-off, etc.), sole proprietorship status.

Dissolution, Liquidation & Bankruptcy

Dissolution: opens the liquidation period. By operation of law (fixed term ends; capital falls below legal minimum) or by shareholders' meeting vote (e.g., losses reduce net worth to less than half of share capital). Liquidation: company retains legal personality but adds "en liquidación" to its name. Liquidators replace administrators — collect credits, pay debts, distribute remainder. Bankruptcy (concurso de acreedores): declaration does not automatically dissolve the company, but if a liquidation phase opens within bankruptcy proceedings, dissolution is automatic and the bankruptcy administrator takes over the liquidator's role.

Competition & Industrial Property Law — Competencia y Propiedad Industrial

▾
📌 Summary
  • Competition Law (Law 15/2007): protects the market structure, not individual competitors. Targets antitrust practices — cartels, abuse of dominant position, unjustified State aid.
  • Unfair Competition (Law 3/1991): regulates conduct in commercial traffic. Any act objectively contrary to good faith is unfair — deception, confusion, servile imitation, exploitation of others' reputation.
  • Industrial Property: protects technical creations and distinctive signs. Requires registration with the SPTO (Spanish Patent & Trademark Office). Key: patents (20 years), trademarks, industrial design.
  • Intellectual Property: protects literary, artistic, and scientific creations. No registration needed — arises automatically. Grants moral rights (authorship, integrity) + economic rights (copyright/reproduction). Corpus mysticum vs. corpus mechanicum.

Market Economy & Free Competition

The market economy rests on entrepreneurial freedom. Competition Law does not protect a specific competitor — it guarantees the proper functioning of the market for all participants. Two axes: (1) Antitrust (Law 15/2007): targets agreements to exclude competitors, abuse of dominant position, unjustified public aid, and excessive concentrations; (2) Promotion of competitiveness: investment in innovation generates intangible assets requiring legal protection.

Unfair Competition — Competencia Desleal (Law 3/1991)

Governs behaviour in commercial traffic — any act objectively contrary to the requirements of good faith is unfair. Key acts:

  • Deception: spreading false or misleading information that distorts economic decisions of the average consumer.
  • Confusion: preventing consumers from distinguishing an original product from an imitation — risk of association on business origin.
  • Imitation and exploitation of reputation: free imitation is the principle, but it becomes illicit when it constitutes a servile copy that takes undue advantage of another's effort or industrial reputation.

Industrial Property — Propiedad Industrial

Focuses on technical creations and distinctive signs. Protection requires registration with the SPTO (Oficina Española de Patentes y Marcas).

  • Patents (Law 11/1986): exclusive exploitation rights for 20 years for inventions with industrial application. Also: utility models (improvements to existing objects).
  • Trademarks (Law 17/2001): distinctive signs that differentiate products/services in the market. Also: commercial name (identifies the entrepreneur); establishment sign.
  • Industrial Design: protects the aesthetic appearance of a product — key for commercial differentiation.

Intellectual Property — Propiedad Intelectual

Protects literary, artistic, or scientific creations. Unlike industrial property, no registration required — rights arise from the act of creation. Key distinction:

Corpus mysticum vs. Corpus mechanicum: Copyright is independent of the physical object. The corpus mysticum = the idea/creation (protected). The corpus mechanicum = the physical support (book, painting, hard drive). Buying a book does not grant copyright — it only transfers ownership of the physical object.

Two types of rights: (1) Moral rights (derechos morales): recognition of authorship, integrity of the work — inalienable; (2) Economic rights / Copyright (derechos de explotación): reproduction, distribution, public communication, transformation — can be transferred.

Commercial Contracts — Contratos Mercantiles

▾
📌 Summary
  • Commercial contracts offer a special regime for speed and security in economic traffic. Spiritualist principle: no strict form required — proved by any means (witness, document, electronic).
  • Commercial sale (Art. 325 CCo): movable goods acquired with the aim of reselling for profit. Seller's key duties: delivery + conformity of goods. Buyer: pay + examine + notify defects. Incoterms = international rules defining who bears expenses, risks, and customs.
  • Commission contract: commercial mandate. Commission agent acts for the principal. Key features: prohibition of self-entry + retention privilege over goods.
  • Agency contract (Law 12/1992): continuous/stable independent intermediary who promotes acts of commerce. Based on intuitu personae + good faith. On termination: possible clientele compensation (indemnización por clientela).
  • Security title (título-valor): a document incorporating a private right, so that its exercise and transfer require possession of the document. Types: nominative · to order (endorsement) · to bearer.

Commercial Sale — Compraventa Mercantil (Art. 325 CCo)

Commercial when: (1) movable goods; (2) acquired with aim of reselling; (3) profit motive. At international level, the Vienna Convention (1980) governs: seller transfers ownership of tangible movable goods in exchange for a price.

  • Seller's obligations: deliver goods (make available at agreed time/place — this transfers risk to buyer); guarantee conformity (quantity, quality, type, packaging); deliver documents (invoices, insurance, certificates of origin).
  • Buyer's obligations: pay price (market price applies if not expressly fixed); receive goods; examine as soon as possible and notify any lack of conformity within a reasonable time.
  • Incoterms (ICC): e.g., EXW, FOB, CIF. International rules defining who bears expenses, risks, transport, and customs. When incorporated into the contract, they acquire the force of law between parties — lex mercatoria.

Agent vs. Distributor: The agent promotes acts in the principal's name/account (earns commission). The distributor buys merchandise in their own name and resells it at their own risk — profit comes from the commercial margin, not a commission. This is a key distinction in commercial law.

Electronic Contracts

Defined by consent expressed through automatic devices or telematic networks. Spanish rule: consent exists from the moment acceptance is manifested (immediate, unlike inter absentes rule for paper contracts). Electronic documentation — e-CMR (electronic consignment note) and electronic bill of lading — have the same probative value as paper if integrity is guaranteed by reliable electronic signatures.

Security Title — Título-Valor

A document incorporating a private right such that the exercise and transfer of that right require possession of the document. Economic function: facilitates circulation of credit and property rights. Three categories by circulation type: (1) Nominative — designates a specific person; transmission requires notification to the issuer; (2) To order (a la orden) — transferable by endorsement; (3) To bearer (al portador) — transmitted by simple physical delivery.

In freight transport, the bill of lading (conocimiento de embarque) is paradigmatic: it proves the contract of carriage, proves receipt of goods on board, and grants the legitimate holder the exclusive right to claim delivery at the destination port. Possession = legal possession of the goods in transit.

Corporate Social Responsibility — RSC / CSR

▾
📌 Summary
  • CSR: voluntary integration of social and environmental concerns into business operations and stakeholder relations. Companies as "corporate citizens."
  • Triple Income Statement: balance of economic profitability + social welfare improvement + environmental preservation. Replaces the shareholder-only model.
  • ISO 26000 (2010): international CSR guidance standard. Not a certifiable standard — a voluntary guide. Universal applicability (all organizations). Principles: human rights, ILO conventions, UN Global Compact.
  • Fiscal responsibility: companies must pay fair taxes in each jurisdiction. Tax transparency required by Law 11/2018 (country-by-country reporting). Avoiding tax havens = CSR commitment.
  • Green taxes: "polluter pays" principle. Companies bear economic responsibility for environmental damage (CO₂ emissions, natural resource impact). Environmental Liability Law requires repair of damage independently of other sanctions.

CSR vs. RSC — Scope

While RSC (Responsabilidad Social Corporativa) typically applies to business activity and SMEs, CSR has broader scope including government agencies and other institutions. Both transcend profit-seeking to integrate social and environmental concerns voluntarily into operations and stakeholder relations. Driven by social distrust following crises and corruption cases.

ISO 26000 (2010) — Key Features

  • Voluntary: not certifiable. Encourages commitment without limiting creativity.
  • Multilateral creation: developed by SIS (Sweden) + ABNT (Brazil), with industry, government, workers, consumers, and NGOs.
  • Universal: applicable to all organizations regardless of size, location, or sector.

Main objectives: help establish SR frameworks; support demonstration of commitment to stakeholders; promote transparency; analyse viability issues while respecting local laws. Built on the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, ILO conventions, UN Global Compact, and Rio Declaration on Environment and Development.

Benefits: increased stakeholder trust; globally accepted single standard; supply chain management emphasis; tool for sustainable development and competitiveness.

Fiscal Responsibility

Companies have a civic duty to pay fair taxes in each jurisdiction according to value generated. Tax compliance finances the Welfare State (health, education, infrastructure) from which the company itself benefits. Law 11/2018 requires country-by-country tax reporting for transparency. Socially responsible companies avoid tax havens and aggressive tax planning — understanding that erosion of social assets weakens market confidence and long-term sustainability.

Green Taxes & Environmental CSR

"Polluter Pays" Principle: Environmental taxes make companies assume economic responsibility for damage caused — CO₂ emissions, impact on soil/water. The Environmental Liability Law requires companies to repair damage independently of other sanctions. CSR-integrated companies use these as drivers of innovation: eco-design, energy efficiency, circular economy → reduce environmental impact AND tax burden.
